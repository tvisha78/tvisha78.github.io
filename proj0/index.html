Part 1 Selfie: The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

Take a picture of yourself from close up. You get a typical distorted selfie image. Now step back several feet from your subject, zoom in, and take a second picture. Try to get the face in the second photo to be the same size as in the first photo.

Close up selfie

Step back and zoom in

Key Takeaway: A close up photo exaggerates facial proportions, making the face appear stretched or distorted. For example, in my close-up selfie, my face looks vertically elongated. Stepping back and zooming in keeps the facial features the same size in the frame, but uses a longer focal length which compresses the features and produces a more natural, proportionate portrait.

Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression

Pick a nice view down a long street (or a walking path on campus), zoom in, and take a photo. Now, walk down the street in the direction of your first shot, and take a second photo without zoom, such that the scene in the two photos appears approximately the same size.

Zoom in on the scene

Without zoom; walk towards the scene

Key Takeaway: When we zoom in from a distance, the camera compresses the scene. Objects that are far apart in real life, like a tree and a bridge, appear closer together in the image. This compression makes the scene look flattened, as if it loses some of the depth and spatial information. In the second photo, taken closer without zoom, objects keep their true distances, preserving natural perspective and depth.

Part 3: The Dolly Zoom

Create a Dolly Zoom by moving the camera back while zooming in, then combine the photos into a GIF. This is a Dolly Zoom of this cute squishie!

Key Takeaway: In a dolly zoom, the subject maintains a fixed size and position across photos, but as we move back and keep zooming in, we alter the background's perspective and depth, making the background seem to approach the subject.
